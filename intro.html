Toxic mould

Toxic mould is dangerous and we think you should learn about how to control it. This site can help you get started.

Medical doctors generally only worry about mould in immune suppressed patients in hospital settings. This situation exists partly because mould infections are difficult to diagnose using routine pathology in the local clinic setting. In GP offices doctors mostly diagnose infections on the basis of anti-body responses detected in blood tests. However when toxic mould enters the body it discards its outer protein coat and displays an inner polysaccharide coat that does not contain proteins to which the immune system can attach antibodies.

Mould can infect your body in many places - the skin, nose, mouth, sinus, bowels, brain and internal organs. Fungi can colonise the membrane surfaces and fluids in body cavities and can spread into the vascular system when the blood is rich in sugar (as in diabetes) and the immune system is compromised.

How can mould survive inside your body?

Fungi can live inside your body by producing mycotoxins and VOCs (volatile organic compounds) that either directly block or corrupt your immune system. While mould can surround itself with toxic chemicals to keep the local immune cells at arms length, it much prefers to have the immune system protect it.

How can mould get the immune system to protect it?

The author, using bioresonance technology, is confronted on a daily basis however with clear signs of immune system corruption by toxic mould in many clients. Toxic mould can use chemical toxins to induce the regulatory cells of the immune system near its colonies to release suppressive cytokine proteins like TGFbeta, VEGF and Interleukin-10. These cytokines quieten down the germ-killing WBCs in the area which then leave the mould colonies alone.

The level of these proteins can be measured in your blood stream and you could ask your doctor to order tests for TGFbeta, VEGF and IL-10 although the cost of testing can be quite high. If one of these results is high the test could be repeated in a few weeks to see if the level remains high. If the result is again high it is possible that you have a toxic mould problem.

The spread is largely into the vascular system and involves the major arteries. First is often the gastric or pancreatico-duodenal arteries. The aorta, common iliac, femoral, pulmonary, vertebral and carotid arteries are favoured as is the Circle of Willis in the brain. Large veins are also colonised - the vena cavae, hepatic, splenic, pulmonary, jugular and subclavian veins are popular colony sites.

The most toxic moulds

The most problematic fungi are:
- Aspergillus : a food storage mould(1r)
- Fusarium : a field fungus(1r)
- Candida albicans: a yeast that produces the carcinogenic chemical acetaldehyde when it invades the body(1br).

Aspergillus and Fusarium species can each produce more than 30 mycotoxins, some of which are potent immune suppressants(2r). These toxins enable mould to survive inside our bodies especially if there are faults with our immune systems.

The commonest fault is a takeover of the spleen engineered by one or both of these fungi often with the help of Candida albicans. Up to 15 different mycotoxins and volatile organic compounds (VOCs) may have to be simultaneously released in order to overwhelm the spleen immune cells but these moulds can combine their resources to do this. Once the spleen has gone down the numbers of mature T cells and Natural Killer (NK) cells steadily drops and the moulds can spread deep into the body.

Other toxic moulds include Eurotium, Chaetomium and Stachybotris. However they lack the survival ability of the top three and will not be much discussed.
